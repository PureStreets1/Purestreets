## What it is
The Felix Project is London's largest food-redistribution charity: it rescues
surplus food and delivers it free to 1,200+ charities and schools, powered by
~18,000 volunteers. A logistics-heavy operation made warm and human on the
surface. (Live site verified — thefelixproject.org now redirects to felix.org;
scale corroborated by ReLondon case study.)

## What works
- **Type:** Sans-serif with substantial-weight headlines; benefit-first phrasing
  ("Rescuing food for fuller lives") and plain section labels ("Our work in
  numbers") — no jargon, no ornament.
- **Restraint:** Airy layout, illustrated icons with breathing room, one clear
  message per band — approachable to a very mixed audience.
- **Colour:** A confident green brand (vans, map) with warm whites/neutrals —
  trust and growth without looking corporate.
- **Density:** Generous spacing prevents the data-heavy story from overwhelming.
- **Photography/illustration:** Documentary photography (people eating, depot
  work) deliberately mixed with flat, warm, custom illustration (fruit tree,
  family at a table) — the illustration humanises the logistics and dodges
  stock-photo sterility.
- **Voice:** Visitor as agent — "How can we help you?" routes people to the right
  next step; impact stated in human terms (people supported, not just tonnage).

## One pattern to adapt
Introduce a **small, warm custom-icon/illustration system** for utilitarian
content. Felix proves a couple of friendly flat illustrations turn a dry
operation human. PureStreets' four numbered Aims (currently bare "01–04") are the
obvious candidate: one simple line/flat icon each (stewardship, neighbours,
wellbeing, action) lifts them from a list into a small visual set — lightweight,
inline, no heavy assets. Flag as a proposal since the owner controls imagery.
