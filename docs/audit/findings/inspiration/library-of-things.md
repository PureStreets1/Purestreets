## What it is
Library of Things (UK social enterprise, founded 2014 in South London) lets
neighbours borrow useful items — drills, sewing machines, sound systems — from
self-service lockers in libraries and community centres. A community/mutual-aid
model wrapped in genuinely design-led, user-tested UX. (Live site verified;
design work corroborated by agency Seymourpowell.)

## What works
- **Type:** Sans-serif; large display-weight headlines in a conversational,
  question-led voice ("Why buy when you can rent?"). Restrained uppercase —
  sentence case is the default for approachability.
- **Restraint:** Calm, near-neutral palette on a light ground; accents kept
  minimal so the eco-conscious, uncluttered feel matches the message.
- **Density:** Airy with clear step-by-step hierarchy; the page teaches as it
  scrolls.
- **Colour/photography:** Warm product photography (the actual Things) mixed with
  illustrated team imagery and real founder/member faces — credible, human, local.
- **Motion:** Light interaction cues (loading states, "Join to get started"
  prompts) rather than showy animation.
- **Voice:** Collective and values-forward — "Together we're saying no to buying
  new" — practical benefit and ethics in the same breath.

## One pattern to adapt
Add a **crisp three-step "how it works" strip** that demystifies taking part.
Library of Things converts a slightly unfamiliar act (borrowing from a locker)
into "browse → reserve → pick up → return". PureStreets could mirror this for a
first-timer's biggest worry — what actually happens at a pickup — with a simple
"Show up → We hand you gear → Pick & log it" module near the top. One small
clarity module removes the main reason newcomers hesitate.
